**CHAPTER 1**
**THE PROBLEM AND ITS BACKGROUND**
**Background of the Study**
The food service industry worldwide is experiencing fundamental changes as digital communication technologies reshape how businesses interact with customers. Among the most notable developments is the rise of chatbot-based ordering systems, which provide food establishments of all sizes with practical tools for streamlining everyday transactions. These systems represent more than a technological upgrade; they signal a broader shift in how micro and small-scale food businesses can leverage automation without requiring substantial capital investment or technical infrastructure. For smaller establishments operating with limited resources, the appeal of such systems lies primarily in their ability to handle repetitive customer interactions efficiently while maintaining accessibility and ease of use. This convergence of automation capability and operational simplicity has positioned conversational ordering platforms as increasingly viable solutions for businesses seeking to improve service delivery without fundamentally altering their existing workflows or customer relationships.
The global landscape of the food service industry is undergoing a significant transformation driven by advances in digital communication technology and the rapid     proliferation of conversational platforms. At the forefront of this transformation is the emergence of chatbot-assisted ordering systems, which offer food businesses—regardless of scale—an accessible means of automating routine transactional processes. Marcineková et al. (2025) conducted a case study on the deployment of an AI-powered chatbot in a Slovak micro-enterprise and found that the system substantially reduced average response times and enabled round-the-clock customer service availability across multiple communication channels. Customers reported high satisfaction levels, citing rapid communication, ease of information retrieval, and a perception of immediacy that manual messaging could not replicate. The study concluded that for micro-enterprises, the primary benefit of chatbot adoption is not advanced analytics but rather the automation of repetitive customer interactions—what the authors termed "agility enablement." Similarly, Panigrahi et al. (2023) employed structural equation modeling with 246 responses from Indian manufacturing SMEs to demonstrate that AI chatbots positively influence supply chain visibility and innovation capability, both of which in turn enhance overall sustainable supply chain performance. Their empirical findings, grounded in dynamic capability theory and analyzed using SmartPLS 4.0, confirm that chatbot adoption in SMEs is a viable strategy for improving operational efficiency even in resource-constrained environments.
In the specific context of food ordering, Jalaludin et al. (2023) developed and deployed a web-based food ordering system integrated with a chatbot for a university cafeteria in Malaysia. Their system replaced a paper-based queue process and effectively eliminated congestion during peak hours while allowing customers to place orders without physical interaction. The study validated that even a modest chatbot integration in a small-scale food setting can yield measurable improvements in transaction speed, order accuracy, and user convenience. These outcomes align closely with the practical goals of the current study. At the consumer adoption level, Romero-Charneco et al. (2025) surveyed 386 active users of a WhatsApp-based restaurant recommendation chatbot in Spain and found that effort expectancy, hedonic motivation, price value, and established platform habits were the most significant predictors of continued chatbot use. Crucially, the study demonstrated that adopting a familiar messaging platform—rather than a standalone application—substantially reduces the learning curve and lowers resistance to adoption, especially among non-tech-savvy users. Complementing these insights, Bonfanti et al. (2023) examined service quality expectations among Italian users of online food ordering and delivery services through four qualitative focus groups. Their findings revealed three core dimensions of customer expectations: the basicness of expectations (ranging from implicit to explicit), the accuracy of expectations (from fuzzy to precise), and the attainability of expectations (from realistic to unrealistic). The authors argued that food businesses must proactively manage these dimensions through customer reassurance, continuous improvement, and flexibility—management strategies that are most effectively supported by a structured digital ordering system. Furthermore, Adiyono et al. (2025) validated the Rapid Application Development (RAD) method as an effective framework for constructing web-based service systems, noting that its emphasis on speed, iterative prototyping, and close user collaboration makes it particularly well-suited for projects requiring rapid delivery and ongoing user input in dynamic service environments—a characteristic that closely mirrors the development context of the current study. Together, these international studies establish a robust evidence base for the global convergence toward chatbot-assisted, platform-integrated food ordering as the dominant operational model for micro-scale food businesses.
In the Philippine context, the relationship between digital engagement and actual business process automation presents what scholars have described as a structural paradox. Filipino MSMEs rank among the most active users of social media platforms globally, yet their integration of digital tools into core business operations remains shallow and uneven. Casimiro et al. (2022) investigated this phenomenon through a survey of 300 respondents and found that the factors most strongly influencing purchase intention on Facebook Messenger were informativeness, assurance, and empathy—qualities that suggest Filipino consumers do not merely seek transactional efficiency but rather desire a relational, conversational experience when engaging with local businesses online. This finding positions Facebook Messenger not simply as a marketing channel but as a primary commercial interface for Filipino consumers, making it a logical foundation for an ordering management system. Cordova et al. (2025) reinforced this picture through a mixed-method study of Philippine MSMEs, which revealed that while businesses have widely adopted social media for marketing purposes, more sophisticated digital tools such as cloud computing platforms and enterprise resource planning systems remain largely inaccessible due to financial constraints, insufficient technical expertise, and deeply ingrained cultural resistance to formalized processes. The study concluded that digital transformation in the Philippine MSME sector is not simply a technological challenge but a socio-cultural one that requires solutions designed around existing behaviors rather than against them.
Briones et al. (2023) further contextualized this landscape through a rapid assessment of digital technology adoption in Philippine agri-food systems, conducted under the Philippine Institute for Development Studies. Their findings indicate that while certain digital tools—such as advisory applications and online retail networks—have gained traction among Filipino smallholder enterprises, many others remain at the prototype stage or in limited deployment. The study warned of an intensifying "digital divide" that risks leaving vulnerable rural businesses further behind and recommended prioritizing "lowest common denominator" technologies that are accessible across all connectivity levels. This recommendation has direct implications for the design of the current system, as TextToEat is intentionally built on platforms—SMS and Messenger—that require no new application downloads and function on basic mobile data connections. Flores and Matias (2025) argued, in a related vein, that digital marketing activities are largely performative unless they are connected to actual process management tools. Their study found that micro-enterprises which invest in digital presence without a corresponding investment in digital operations fail to achieve sustained financial efficiency. Meanwhile, Lao (2023) documented a pervasive "collective resistance" to digital payment systems among Filipino micro-enterprise owners and customers, driven primarily by security concerns and unfamiliarity with cashless transaction protocols. This behavioral pattern directly informs a key design decision in the current study: the deliberate exclusion of digital payment gateways from TextToEat in favor of preserving the eatery's established cash-based transaction model, ensuring that the system augments operations without triggering adoption resistance.
At the local level, the province of Ilocos Sur presents a compelling microcosm of the national patterns documented above, with its own distinctive characteristics shaped by regional culture, economic structure, and community commerce traditions. Pascua et al. (2023) assessed the financial management practices of 182 micro-business owners and managers across the different cities and municipalities of Ilocos Sur and found that the status of financial management among these businesses was only at a "moderate" level. The study revealed a heavy reliance on informal bookkeeping practices, with financial planning, budgeting, and accounts management carried out through mental arithmetic and paper-based ledgers. Inventory management was among the more practiced activities, yet cash and receivables management remained inconsistent. This finding directly describes the operational reality of Lacasandile Eatery, where order tracking, bill computation, and inventory management are similarly conducted through informal means—specifically, through manual back-reading of a Facebook Messenger group chat. Banzil et al. (2025) investigated the chichacorn industry in Candon City, Ilocos Sur, and similarly found that while local food enterprises demonstrate high production quality and craftsmanship, the management side of these businesses frequently lacks modernization. The absence of systematic tools for order management, costing, and inventory creates inefficiencies that constrain the growth potential of otherwise capable enterprises.
Quidulit (2025) analyzed the marketing management strategies of local food industries in Sta. Maria, Ilocos Sur, from a customer perspective and found that while customers value digital visibility and appreciate businesses that maintain an active online presence, they continue to prioritize ease of purchase and convenience in their actual buying behavior. The study noted that customers in the region remain strongly oriented toward cash-based transactions and traditional ordering methods, a preference that any digital ordering solution must accommodate rather than attempt to replace. Divina and Rodiris (2025) further found that business owners in Ilocos Sur exhibit notably high levels of digital literacy, yet their application of this literacy is confined almost entirely to communication activities such as messaging and social media posting, rather than extended to process automation or digital management. This gap—between digital awareness and digital utilization—is the precise condition that motivates the development of TextToEat: a system designed to leverage the communication habits that business owners already possess and redirect them toward structured, manageable operational workflows. Absolor et al. (2025) offered a critical cultural dimension to this discussion, arguing that any innovation introduced into the local enterprise ecosystem of Ilocos Sur must respect and reflect the cultural identity of the business. Their study on the cultural significance of One Town, One Product (OTOP) enterprises in the province highlights that community-centric eateries in Ilocos Sur are not merely commercial entities but cultural institutions embedded in the social fabric of their communities. A system that introduces rigid, impersonal interfaces would be incongruent with this cultural context; conversely, a conversational, chat-based ordering system aligns organically with the relational nature of community commerce in the region. The synthesis of these global, national, and local studies reveals a coherent and convergent research gap. Internationally, evidence strongly supports chatbot-assisted ordering as a viable and effective automation solution for micro-scale food businesses. At the national level, the Philippine context confirms both the widespread reliance on messaging platforms like Facebook Messenger as commercial interfaces and the formidable socio-cultural barriers that prevent the adoption of complex digital tools. Locally, Ilocos Sur data establishes that business owners in the region are digitally literate but operationally informal, that customers prioritize transactional ease and cash-based payment, and that cultural identity must be respected in any technological intervention. There is currently no existing tool that bridges the gap between these realities—that is, no system that provides the structural capability of a database-backed order management platform while preserving the informal, conversational familiarity of a group chat. This is precisely the gap that TextToEat: An SMS and Facebook-Messenger Based Order Management System for Lacasandile Eatery is designed to fill, offering Lacasandile Eatery a solution that is simultaneously simple enough to adopt, powerful enough to automate, and culturally appropriate enough to sustain.
**Conceptual Framework of the Study**
The study employs the Input-Process-Output (IPO) Model as its conceptual framework, as shown in Figure 1. The Input component includes the essential data needed to process customer transactions such as the eatery’s daily menu, pricing, and meal availability levels, as well as customer order details and contact information received through SMS, Messenger, or web-based channels. The Process component is the TextToEat System, which facilitates chatbot-assisted ordering, validates orders by checking meal availability, confirms customer requests, and automatically records transactions while computing order totals. The Output component consists of organized and retrievable customer orders, accurate billing results, updated meal availability information, transaction logs, and daily order summary reports.

# Objectives of the Study
The general objective is to develop TextToEat: An SMS and Facebook-Messenger Based Order Management System for Lacasandile Eatery that automates ordering tasks for Lacasandile Eatery. The system aims to minimize manual errors, reduce response delays, and enhance overall order management efficiency.
Specifically, this study aimed to:

- Determine the current traditional operational challenges experienced by Lacasandile Eatery, particularly in terms of order retrieval, bill calculation, and overall order management using manual and group chat-based methods.
- Identify the features integrated into the proposed SMS and Messenger-based ordering system, focusing on how the system automates customer inquiries and supports order processing to maximize operational efficiency and reduce workload for the owner and staff.
- Evaluate the level of acceptability of the proposed system among the eatery’s owner, staff, and customers through the use of the System Usability Scale (SUS)**,** in order to assess the system’s usability, usefulness, and overall user satisfaction.

**Scope and Limitation of the Study**
The study focuses on the development and implementation of the TextToEat: An SMS and Facebook-Messenger Based Order Management System for Lacasandile Eatery specifically for Lacasandile Eatery in Ilocos Sur. The authorized users of the system include the owner and staff, who access a simplified order monitoring interface (e.g., Kitchen List), and customers** **who place orders through SMS and Messenger. The system primarily **s**upport**s **chatbot-assisted customer inquiries, order placement, automated transaction logging, meal availability checking, and total bill computation. This scope was selected to address the eatery’s difficulty in handling manual order processing and retrieving customer requests during peak hours.
The study is limited by the exclusion of in-app digital payment gateways to align with the eatery’s cash-based transaction preference. Thus, the system functions as an ordering and transaction recording tool rather than a payment processing platform. Additionally, the system does not include voice-to-text features**, **image-based ordering, or advanced artificial intelligence functions. The system is designed for single eatery use only and is not intended for multi-branch deployment. Lastly, the system’s performance depends on the stability of telecommunication services and third-party messaging platforms such as Facebook Messenger, which may affect service delivery.
**Importance of the Study**
The study provides significant benefits to specific stakeholders. **The owner and staff** benefit from the elimination of manual backreading in group chats and calculation errors, allowing them to focus on food preparation and customer service quality. **Customers** enjoy faster transactions and instant, accurate digital receipts without waiting for confirmation. **The College and the Department** gain a documented case study showcasing practical technology applications in local enterprise development. **Researchers** gain practical insights into the application of Conversational UI (CUI) in micro-enterprises, particularly in understanding how informal business operations can be formalized through accessible technology platforms, while **future researchers** can explore how automation can be integrated into small-scale food businesses like karinderyas, which operate differently from larger restaurants. Overall, this study demonstrates how a simple conversational ordering system can streamline operations without disrupting the traditional "tingi" practice or the community-oriented nature of local eateries that is deeply embedded within the Filipino food culture. Moreover, it provides a replicable model for similar community-based food establishments seeking digital transformation while preserving their cultural authenticity and operational traditions, thereby contributing to sustainable local economic development and the broader digital inclusion agenda for Philippine micro-enterprises. Furthermore, this research enriches academic literature on appropriate technology design for informal economies and validates the effectiveness of low-barrier digital solutions in resource-constrained business environments.

**METHODOLOGY**
This chapter discusses the procedures that will be followed in order to gather the necessary information for the study and conduct the required analysis. It presents the overall research methodology that will be used in developing and evaluating the proposed system. Specifically, this chapter describes the research design, software development model, project plan, project assignments, population and locale of the study, research instruments, and data analysis techniques to be utilized. It also explains how the respondents will be selected, how data will be collected from the target stakeholders, and how the gathered responses will be processed and interpreted to address the objectives of the study.
Research Design
This study will utilize a descriptive-developmental research design. Descriptive research aims to systematically and accurately describe existing conditions, practices, and problems without manipulating the environment. This design is appropriate for the study because it will enable the researchers to identify and document the current operational challenges experienced by Lacasandile Eatery, particularly in terms of order retrieval, bill calculation, and overall order management. Through observation and documentation, the study will capture how the eatery currently processes orders using group chats and manual methods, especially during peak hours when high message volume significantly compromises order tracking and staff efficiency."
In addition, the study will apply the developmental component of the research design, which focuses on the design, development, and evaluation of tools, systems, or processes through a structured and iterative approach. Developmental research is commonly used in system-based studies because it supports the creation of a working solution while continuously improving it through feedback and testing. In the context of this study, the descriptive-developmental approach will allow the researchers not only to describe the existing order handling process of the eatery but also to design, develop, and evaluate the proposed TextToEat: An SMS and Facebook-Messenger Based Order Management System for Lacasandile Eatery. This approach will ensure that the developed system is based on real operational needs and that its usability and acceptability can be measured using a standardized evaluation tool.
**Software Model**
The Rapid Application Development (RAD) Model will be adopted as the software development methodology for this project. RAD emphasizes fast prototyping, active user involvement, and iterative cycles that produce a functional system quickly and allow continuous improvement based on stakeholder feedback. This model is widely used in information systems research and development to shorten delivery time while maintaining quality, and it has been applied successfully in recent academic studies involving information system design projects (Adiyono et al., 2025).
The RAD process includes four main phases: Requirements Planning, where essential needs will be gathered from stakeholders; User Design/Prototyping, where a prototype of TextToEat with key interfaces will be developed and presented to users for feedback; Construction/Iterative Development, where improvements will be made rapidly based on feedback; and Cutover/Implementation, where the system will be deployed and final adjustments will be made based on actual usage. This flow supports rapid delivery and will ensure the system effectively meets the operational needs of the eatery.

**Project Plan**
The project plan for the development of the TextToEat system is presented in Figure 3, covering the period from January 2026 to February 2026. The schedule follows the Rapid Application Development (RAD) Model, which emphasizes fast prototyping, active user involvement, and iterative development to deliver a functional system within a short timeframe. This approach is appropriate for a small-scale eatery like Lacasandile Eatery, where continuous feedback from the owner and staff is crucial to ensure usability and practicality.
During the Requirements Planning phase, system requirements will be gathered from the owner and staff. This will in**volve** mapping the eatery’s current workflow and identifying common issues during peak hours, particularly in order retrieval, bill calculation, and overall order management. Key requirements will include the daily menu, pricing, meal availability monitoring, and preferred customer ordering channels via SMS and Messenger. This will ensure that the system’s features align with the eatery’s operational needs.
In the User Design/Prototyping phase, an initial working prototype of TextToEat will be developed and presented to stakeholders. The staff-side interface will include an order queue to track incoming orders, supporting smoother coordination and workflow in the kitchen.
The Construction/Iterative Development phase will refine the prototype based on feedback from the owner and staff. Adjustments will be made to improve transaction logging, enhance meal availability checks, streamline system flow, and boost overall usability. Multiple iterations will ensure the system remains practical, user-friendly, and suited for small-scale operations.
During the Cutover/Implementation phase in February 2026, the system will be deployed for actual use. Customers will place orders via SMS or Messenger, while staff will manage them through the dashboard. The owner and staff will be oriented on features such as viewing the menu, marking items as sold-out, managing the order queue, monitoring meal availability, and accessing sales records. Minor troubleshooting will also be performed to ensure smooth operation under real conditions.
Finally, the Evaluation and Documentation phase will assess the system’s overall usability and acceptability. The System Usability Scale (SUS) questionnaire will be administered to the owner, staff, and selected customers, and responses will be analyzed to identify areas for improvement. The final documentation will consolidate the results, update system descriptions, and finalize the manuscript based on findings from implementation and testing.

**Population and Locale of the Study**
This study will be conducted at Lacasandile Eatery in Ilocos Sur, Philippines, a small local eatery that primarily relies on group chat messaging and manual methods for handling customer orders. The eatery’s current process involves receiving customer requests through chat, manually listing orders, checking meal availability, and computing total bills using traditional methods. Due to the volume of messages during peak hours, the eatery experiences challenges in tracking orders, retrieving customer requests, and ensuring accurate bill computation. For this reason, the study will focus on developing a SMS and Messenger-based system that is suitable for the operational context of a small-scale community eatery.
The population of the study will consist of the eatery’s owner, staff members, and a sample of regular customers who actively participate in the ordering process. These respondents will be purposively selected because they represent the primary stakeholders who will directly use and benefit from the proposed TextToEat system. The owner and staff will serve as key informants in identifying operational challenges and validating system features, while the customers will provide feedback regarding the ordering experience through SMS and Messenger. The respondents will participate in prototype testing and system interaction activities, and they will later be included in the usability evaluation through the administration of the System Usability Scale (SUS). The collected responses will be used to determine the overall acceptability of the system in terms of ease of use, effectiveness, and practicality for Lacasandile Eatery’s daily operations. Additionally, IT experts will evaluate the system's technical design and implementation quality.

**Research Instruments**
The study will employ various instruments to gather the necessary qualitative and quantitative data needed to address the objectives of the research. First, observation and documentation forms will be used during the Requirements Planning phase to systematically record the existing operational practices of Lacasandile Eatery. These forms will capture key details such as the daily menu list, pricing, meal availability handling, and the step-by-step order process currently performed through group chat and manual listing. This instrument will also help document common challenges experienced during peak hours, including missed messages, delayed order retrieval, and manual computation errors.
Additionally, system prototype evaluation forms will be utilized during the User Design/Prototyping and Construction/Iterative Development phases to gather structured feedback from the eatery’s owner, staff, and selected customers. The evaluation forms will include guide questions and rating items focusing on the usability and clarity of the system interface. Specifically, they will assess the ease of using the dashboard features such as menu viewing, search functions, sold-out indicators, and the staff-side order queue. They will also include items related to perceived usefulness, such as whether the prototype helps improve order tracking, reduces confusion, and supports faster order handling. The feedback collected through this instrument will serve as the basis for iterative improvements and revisions of the system.
Finally, the study will utilize the System Usability Scale (SUS) as the main instrument for evaluating the overall acceptability and usability of the TextToEat system. The SUS is a standardized and validated tool widely used in system evaluation research. It consists of a 10-item Likert-type questionnaire that measures user perceptions regarding ease of use, learnability, confidence in using the system, and overall satisfaction. The SUS results will yield a single usability score that can be interpreted to determine whether the system is acceptable and practical for real users, particularly the owner, staff, and customers of Lacasandile Eatery.
Data Analysis
The data gathered in this study will be analyzed to directly answer the specific research objectives. For Objective 1 and Objective 2, Content Analysis will be applied to the qualitative data obtained from observation, documentation, and prototype feedback. Responses and recorded notes will be grouped into themes related to operational challenges, such as order retrieval difficulties, billing issues, and management concerns, as well as themes related to system requirements and desired features. This method will allow the researcher to organize and interpret the collected information in a systematic manner, supporting the identification of key problems and the justification of the proposed system features.
Finally, Descriptive Statistics will be employed to analyze the results of the System Usability Scale (SUS). The responses will be computed to obtain the overall SUS usability score, and the weighted mean will be used to summarize the acceptability level of the proposed system among the owner, staff, and customers. The computed results will be interpreted using the rating scale presented in Table 3, allowing the study to determine whether TextToEat achieves an acceptable usability level and to identify possible areas for improvement based on user feedback.
